Pit wall

What breaks on the Underground — and can we predict it yet?

Not a museum. This page answers two questions: what should I build next, and is the data any good yet. Everything here is measured from a minute-resolution archive of the live network — none of it is published retrospectively, so it only exists because it's being recorded.

Can we make the call yet?

Each row is a feature the app can't honestly ship until the data supports it. ~20 incidents per cause before a median stops being noise — a rule of thumb, not a law. Forecasts assume incidents keep arriving at the rate they have so far; real life is bursty, so read the range, not the number.

Lead time over TfL — the moat, and the number that could prove us wrong

TfL's status feed is human-curated and therefore lagging: someone decides the line is bad and updates it. Train movements are the leading indicator. Two signals — trains running slow, and trains not running at all. Absence is the stronger one: in a suspension trains don't slow down, they stop, and the gap in the data is the event. If this number isn't positive, the thesis is weaker than we think.

Recent incidents

The trace is the real severity log — timestamped steps, not a drawn curve. TfL's own words are quoted underneath, so you can audit the cause classifier rather than trust it. Planned closures, engineering work and overnight closures are excluded — they are the timetable, not incidents, and they were previously producing 14-hour "disruptions" on lines that simply don't run at night.

By line

"Total minutes disrupted" is a bad metric — it treats a slightly late Circle line the same as a suspended Northern line. So it's split by severity.
